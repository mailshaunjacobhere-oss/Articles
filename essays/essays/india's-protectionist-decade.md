India’s Protectionist Decade; small-scale reflections on a large-scale democracy
The prevailing orthodoxy of the post-Cold War era was built on an almost theological belief in convergence: global economic openness would gradually dissolve the strategic importance of national industrial boundaries. States would integrate into international markets, specialise according to comparative advantage, and eventually discover prosperity through interdependence rather than autonomy. India accepted this settlement later than most, but after its 1991 balance-of-payments crisis, it embraced it with increasing confidence.

For nearly three decades, that confidence appeared justified. The Indian economy expanded rapidly, trade volumes climbed, a new middle class emerged, and the services sector achieved extraordinary global integration. Liberalisation was no longer viewed merely as policy; it became historical common sense.

Yet the past decade marks a visible departure from that consensus. India has not abandoned markets, nor has it retreated into the closed, bureaucratic economic nationalism of the pre-1991 License Raj. What has emerged instead is a more complex phenomenon: an aggressively defensive developmental posture operating from within globalisation itself. This distinction is critical. India’s recent protectionism is not designed to sever integration with the global economy. It was designed to renegotiate the terms under which that integration occurs.

1. The Realities of Structural Dependency

The underlying realisation driving this shift was uncomfortable but increasingly unavoidable: a nation may participate deeply in global markets while remaining structurally dependent within them. Liberalisation integrated India into international circuits of capital and consumption, but it failed to produce sufficient manufacturing sovereignty. The country exported software at scale but remained heavily reliant on imported physical inputs.

This structural asymmetry meant that while India dominated global IT services, it relied almost entirely on foreign supply chains for foundational hardware. The economy remained exposed to critical vulnerabilities across key inputs:

Active Pharmaceutical Ingredients (APIs): Where dependency on a single neighbour, China, hovered near 70% for basic life-saving drugs.
Electronics and Hardware: Overwhelming reliance on imported printed circuit boards (PCBs) and semiconductor ecosystems.
Green Energy Infrastructure: Heavy dependency on foreign solar cells and modules, directly tying national climate goals to international supply chain bottlenecks.

The strategic anxiety produced by this imbalance intensified after 2014 and accelerated dramatically during the COVID-19 pandemic. The global health crisis exposed the extreme fragility of extended, just-in-time supply chains. Simultaneously, the U.S.–China rivalry transformed trade architectures into geopolitical leverage, and subsequent macroeconomic shocks demonstrated how quickly global interdependence could be weaponised into strategic vulnerability. In this environment, self-reliance ceased to appear ideological; it became purely logistical.

India’s response was massive. Over the last decade, the state re-entered industrial policy with force, for the first time since the economic opening. Tariffs on electronic components, communication equipment, and specific industrial machinery were calibrated upward. Domestic procurement requirements tightened, anti-dumping investigations expanded, and production-linked subsidies were introduced on a historic scale. The language of free-market efficiency gradually gave way to the language of resilience, capability, and strategic autonomy.

2. The Illusion of Frictionless Integration

Predictably, orthodox trade theorists interpreted this shift as economic regression. From the perspective of highly liberalised, trade-oriented systems - particularly East and Southeast Asian export networks built on low-friction global integration - India appeared increasingly defensive.

Economies such as Singapore and Taiwan spent decades refining export competitiveness through deep integration into global supply chains, zero-to-low tariff barriers, and relentless exposure to international competition. Even smaller advanced economies like Switzerland represented the opposite developmental instinct: extreme specialisation achieved through radical openness rather than strategic protection.

India explicitly moved in another direction, accepting higher institutional and import friction in exchange for industrial depth. This undeniably imposes immediate domestic costs:

Input Bottlenecks: Indian manufacturers have to frequently make expensive imports, undermining export competitiveness in downstream industries.
Consumer Premium: Domestic consumers absorb price increases in protected sectors; import duties shield local producers from global price discovery.
Regulatory Burden: Foreign investors encounter greater compliance complexity, localised data requirements, and shifting bureaucratic mandates.

While landmark structural reforms like GST successfully dismantled physical state-border check-posts and unified the internal market, they didn't eliminate friction - they digitised it. From trapped working capital of inverted duty structures to intense E-Way bill compliance, the micro-frictions holding back Indian manufacturing have evolved.

Critics argue that India risks repeating the failures of twentieth-century import substitution, where protected industries survived through political insulation rather than technological innovation. Yet, this criticism captures only half the story.

3. Dissecting the PLI Framework: Discipline vs. Rent-Seeking

What makes India’s protectionist decade historically unique is not merely that protection increased, but that it coincided with a rapid attempt to impose export-led manufacturing discipline at scale.

Historically, developmental states succeeded not because they protected domestic industries indefinitely, but because they forced domestic firms to compete internationally after periods of state support. Protection without export discipline degenerates into rent extraction; firms become politically valuable without becoming globally competitive, and the state ends up subsidising survival rather than capability.

The Production Linked Incentive (PLI) framework - spanning critical sectors like electronics, pharmaceuticals, and advanced chemistry cell batteries - reveals the distinction clearly. The objective was not simply to shield domestic producers from foreign competition. The state instead attempted to use selective protection, targeted output-based subsidies, and domestic market scale to accelerate integration into global manufacturing chains while retaining greater national control over production capacity.

This did not occur through free-market spontaneity alone. It emerged through tariff escalation on completely built units (CBUs), production incentives tied strictly to incremental sales milestones, domestic assembly mandates, and geopolitical supply-chain diversification away from China.

Annual smartphone exports under the PLI push have scaled dramatically, crossing tens of billions of dollars, driven heavily by global tech anchors like Apple and Samsung's localised manufacturing operations. Crucially, India did not merely protect domestic consumption markets; it increasingly attempted to discipline firms through export orientation. Manufacturing growth was expected not only to substitute imports but to compete internationally.

4. The Structural Fault Lines of Scale

This is where the Indian experiment diverges both from classical protectionism and from pure neoliberal openness. The older protectionism of the license-permit era emerged from scarcity economics and state suspicion of markets; the newer protectionism emerges from active participation inside markets judged to be strategically unequal. Its objective is not autarky; its objective is leverage.

At the same time, India’s approach remains structurally unstable in ways that are undermined by supporters and critics. Export-led manufacturing discipline cannot survive on tariffs alone. East Asian industrialization succeeded through enormous, coordinated investments in logistics, bureaucratic velocity, port efficiency, technical education, infrastructure reliability, and administrative coherence. Protection was only one component within a far larger developmental architecture.

The Transaction Cost Paradox: Protectionism is a single policy lever; it cannot substitute for deep structural reform. You can levy strategic tariffs, but they will not pave roads, fix municipal bureaucratic delays, or resolve the chronic judicial backlogs that tie up corporate contract disputes for decades.
India still struggles with these underlying conditions. Regulatory fragmentation remains severe, and state capacity varies enormously across regions. Compliance burdens and policy unpredictability continue to raise transaction costs.

There is also the unresolved danger that politically protected sectors may acquire permanent insulation without achieving true technological depth. While smartphone production value has hit record highs domestically, a significant portion remains confined to downstream assembly. Moving upstream into component fabrication, complex tooling, and core intellectual property design requires deep technological capabilities that tariffs alone cannot generate.

Conclusion: The Central Contradiction

This is the central contradiction of India’s protectionist decade. The state is simultaneously attempting to shield domestic industry from external pressure while exposing it to export discipline quickly enough to prevent stagnation.

If protection lasts too long, inefficiency calcifies and creates rent-seeking monopolies.
If exposure occurs too quickly, domestic firms collapse under international competition before capability formation matures.

The balancing act is extraordinarily difficult. And yet, despite these structural constraints, India has managed within a relatively compressed period to reposition itself meaningfully inside global manufacturing conversations. Electronics assembly, defense manufacturing, pharmaceuticals, renewable energy components, and semiconductor ambitions now occupy a far more central place in economic strategy than at any point in the post-1991 era.

Whether this transformation succeeds ultimately depends on one question: Can India convert protected scale into competitive capability?

The answer will determine whether the past decade is remembered as a transitional phase in the emergence of a resilient developmental state, or as another cycle of defensive economic nationalism unable to sustain productivity growth. What is already clear, however, is that the old binaries no longer hold. The contemporary world no longer presents a clean choice between globalization and protectionism. Increasingly, major states are attempting to combine openness with strategic insulation, integration with industrial sovereignty, and market participation with geopolitical resilience.

India’s last decade stands as a primary case study within that larger global transformation. Its significance lies not simply in the return of tariffs or subsidies, but in the re-emergence of a deeper political question that globalization once appeared to settle prematurely: how much economic dependence can a major democracy tolerate before openness itself begins to feel strategically unsafe?
