**In the realm of symbols: how we think, affect and shape the world**
Humans derive pleasure from thinking. This is not a trivial observation, but a clue to a profound cognitive divergence between our species and the rest of the animal kingdom. Evolutionary and cognitive accounts of behavioural modernity suggest that modern human life is marked by a distinctive suite of capacities: abstraction, recursive thought, symbolic communication, and long-range planning. Archaeologists track these capacities through physical artefacts and ritualised behaviours, but their truest monument is the complex social world we have constructed. This cognitive expansion also comes with a built-in cost. A mind capable of abstraction and projection is a mind newly exposed to uncertainty, anticipation, and existential dread. In this sense, the pleasure of thinking is the necessary fuel for a high-stakes, energetically expensive brain - a reward structure that coaxes us to exercise the very capacities that disrupt our peace.

This is what then lies at the core of it: the very powers that elevate us into symbolic life also divide us. There is a persistent, structural tension between the inner life of the self and its outward existence in social, material, and institutional worlds. The thinking, reflecting subject is never fully at home in the order it inhabits. It is trapped in a permanent suspension between inward experience and external constraint, between what it senses itself to be and the rigid categories, roles, and demands through which society recognizes it.

Karl Marx’s critique of capitalism is indispensable here, not merely as a piece of theory, but as a diagnosis of this cognitive fracturing. Alienation, for him, was not a mood but a structure. Capitalist labor estranges human beings from the product of their work, from the activity of labor itself, from one another, and from what Marx called their species-being: the distinctively human capacity for free, conscious, creative, and social activity. What makes this insight especially powerful is that alienation can be read as a deformation of the very capacities associated with behavioral modernity. The industrial workplace does not simply exhaust the body; it captures the human powers of planning, reflection, creativity, and coordination, then narrows them into repetitive and externally directed functions. In that sense, alienation is not only economic dispossession. It is a structural theft of the human capacity to shape the world meaningfully

We cannot escape this trap by simply searching for a flawless social formula, because the relationship between the self and the world is permanently incomplete. The self is not a sealed interior essence confronting a fully external world; it is continuously co-authored by language, norms, and inherited categories of thought. We do not first become selves and then enter symbolic life; we only become intelligible to ourselves through symbolic life. Yet language, the very apparatus that holds us together, is inherently partial. It enables recognition even as it leaves our deepest vulnerabilities unsaid.

Because this symbolic incompleteness can never be resolved once and for all, politics cannot wait for final, timeless certainty. Twentieth-century political philosophy recognized this impasse, shifting its focus away from grand metaphysical truths and toward the urgent, practical design of institutions that might manage our collective volatility. John Rawls exemplifies this transition. His historicist insight reveals that what counts as justice, freedom, or equality is never raised in a vacuum; the questions themselves mutate alongside the societies that ask them. It does not collapse normative thought into helpless relativism. Rather, it proves that our frameworks for justice must remain as fluid and adaptive as the historical conditions they seek to govern.

If our symbolic systems are historically burdened and structurally defective, a just world can never be a finished project. Instead, it requires the aggressive practice of affirmativity: the internal, friction-filled capacity of symbolic systems to revise themselves, when they fail to register lived reality. Affirmativity is not passive optimism, nor do institutions naturally correct themselves. It is a process of institutional self-repair dragged into existence by necessity: the creation of new vocabularies to articulate ignored suffering, the radical revision of legal categories when inherited definitions exclude, and the dismantling of public norms that refuse to recognize the marginalized. Symbolic life is not redeemed by escaping it, but by rendering it fiercely revisable.

This is why the pleasure of thinking is necessarily liberating. Thought is not a luxury for inward satisfaction; it is the primary weapon through which human beings contest the worlds that shape them. The same capacities that expose us to alienation are the exact tools that equip us for critique. To think is to reinterpret, to judge, and to transform a flawed symbolic order. Behavioural modernity and political judgment are not separate domains; they converge in a single, tragic predicament: as symbolic beings, we are uniquely capable of building the very structures that fracture us, which means we must endlessly build and rebuild the worlds in which we seek to be whole. This is, by its very definition, impossible for beings with a finite timeline, and a task for eternity; yet systems we build leave its traces long after we are gone. That alone should help us move with a greater sense of responsibility, and ironically, more humour, than generally prescribed.
