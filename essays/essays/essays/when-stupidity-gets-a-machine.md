**When Stupidity Gets a Machine: Cipolla, AI and the End of Uniqueness**
Human stupidity will outlive its bounds. Not as a joke, but as a warning.

Carlo M. Cipolla did not write about human stupidity in jest. In The Basic Laws of Human Stupidity, first published in Italian in 1976 and later issued by Società editrice il Mulino in Bologna, he treated stupidity as a serious social force rather than a comic defect. What makes the text endure is not merely its wit, but its unnerving suggestion that irrationality is not incidental to collective life; it is constitutive of it, and often ruinous. Cipolla’s framework is deceptively simple. Human action, in his account, may be understood in terms of gains and losses distributed between oneself and others, and the stupid person is the one who causes losses to others while deriving no gain, or even suffering loss, in the process. This is what gives stupidity its singular menace: unlike banditry, exploitation, or ordinary selfishness, it injures without intelligible advantage.

Yet it is precisely here that the old model begins to strain. For Cipolla wrote in a world in which stupidity remained, however destructive, recognizably human. The advent of artificial intelligence compels a revision, not because machines have become human, but because human stupidity has now acquired a technical medium through which it can be preserved, replicated, and scaled. AI must therefore be read not only as a triumph of technical progress, nor merely as the latest chapter in the philosophy of mind, but as an episode in the history of stupidity. This is not a frivolous juxtaposition. It is an attempt to think AI against the background of human irrationality, where its promise and its danger become legible at once. The conceptual disturbance is subtle, but decisive. If the most disastrous category in Cipolla’s scheme is the one that imposes losses both on others and on oneself, then stupidity is marked by a peculiar unity of self-harm and general harm. AI disrupts the uniqueness of that category by introducing forms of systematic error that cannot be reduced to individual folly.

Consider the ordinary litany of AI failure: a model trained on historical prejudice reproduces discrimination at scale; a chatbot hallucinates with confidence, laundering falsehood through the authority of form; an optimization system pursues a measurable target while destroying the unmeasured goods that gave the target meaning. In each case, the harm is distributed outward to users, institutions, and publics, yet it also returns inward. The system corrodes trust in its own outputs, diminishes its utility, and undermines the legitimacy of those who deploy it.

This is why AI is dangerous even to human stupidity. It is not simply that machines inherit our defects; it is that they convert defects into infrastructure. Human stupidity, in its older form, was bounded by the limits of persons, situations, and institutions. Now it is encoded into systems capable of repetition without reflection and scale without judgment. What was once episodic becomes continuous. What was once local becomes planetary.

The point is not that AI is "stupid" in the way a person is stupid. That would be too easy, and perhaps too anthropomorphic. The deeper problem is that AI gives stupidity a new ontology. It displaces stupidity from the sphere of temperament into the sphere of process. Stupidity no longer appears only as a flaw in the acting subject; it emerges as a property of technological arrangements that no individual fully commands and from which no individual can easily withdraw.

This should force a reconsideration of Cipolla’s most unsettling intuition. The old common sense held that causing loss to oneself and to others at once was uniquely stupid. But the uniqueness no longer holds in the same way. The machine age does not abolish stupidity; it pluralizes it. It gives us derivative stupidity, automated stupidity, optimised stupidity, stupidity in feedback loops, stupidity with computational force behind it. 

That change is more serious than it first appears. For if the machine can reproduce patterns of harm without consciousness, then stupidity can persist without intention. If it can scale error without comprehension, then stupidity can become structurally anonymous. One is no longer dealing only with fools, charlatans, and vandals, but with systems whose failures are diffused across code, data, incentives, institutions, and the willing credulity of their users. Here one might object that all AI stupidity is still, in the final analysis, human stupidity. After all, machines do not train themselves ex nihilo; they are built, tuned, and deployed by people. This is partly true, but insufficient. The distinction is not between human and non-human origin, but between humanly caused error and machine-mediated amplification. A prejudice in one person is socially dangerous; a prejudice embedded in a model and operationalized across domains acquires a different order of consequence altogether.

Cipolla remains indispensable because he understood something modern societies prefer to forget: intelligence does not immunize a civilization against stupidity. In fact, complexity may enlarge the field upon which stupidity acts, allowing damage to travel farther before it is even recognized as damage. AI belongs to this paradox. It is a monument to extraordinary intelligence, and for that very reason it can become a more efficient carrier of extraordinary stupidity. The irony is grim. The more sophisticated the tool, the less tolerable the old comfort that folly is merely comic. We may have once imagined stupidity as a human failing best endured with the patience one reserves for vanity, error, or the jester’s licensed excess. That indulgence becomes harder to sustain when stupidity is no longer confined to the erring individual, but instantiated in systems that advise, rank, predict, filter, and decide.

This is why the question before us is not whether AI can think. That question, though important, is no longer the most urgent. The more urgent question is whether intelligence, once externalised into machines, can escape the irrational structure of the world that produced it. There is little reason for confidence. Tools inherit their makers, but they also magnify them. Human stupidity, then, has not been superseded by artificial intelligence. It has found in AI its most formidable accomplice. The machine does not cancel folly; it hardens it into procedure, gives it velocity, and disguises it as competence. What Cipolla diagnosed as a perennial feature of human conduct now threatens to become a standing condition of technological modernity.

If so, then stupidity will indeed outlive its bounds. It will do so not because humanity has failed to invent intelligence, but because it has learned how to mechanize unreason. And that, more than any singular malfunction, is what makes AI a genuinely modern danger.
